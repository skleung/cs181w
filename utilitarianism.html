Ethics

Utilitarianism

Classical utilitarianism considers the action that maximizes utility to be the moral action. To determine the ethical grounds for vigilantism under this standard, one must determine whether these actions promote the same “greatest happiness of the greatest number” that Jeremy Bentham describes. Such an assessment confronts many of the common criticisms of utilitarianism, most notably the claim of impracticality. This criticism of utilitarianism argues that utility cannot be quantified or measured—one cannot, in practice, calculate all the effects for all the individuals. Admittedly, the number of individuals affected by vigilantism makes the utilitarian calculus impossible in many cases.

Consider the actions of Julian Assange, founder of WikiLeaks, through a utilitarian lens. By releasing sensitive government documents that have garnered front-page news coverage, Assange has managed to influence the utility of the American commoner. But does the reality of WikiLeaks offer a greater happiness to the commoner than the ignorance in its absence? A poll by The Washington Post and ABC News found that most Americans (68 percent) say the WikiLeaks’ exposure of government documents harms the public interest. Nearly as many (59 percent) say the U.S. government should arrest Assange and charge him with a crime for releasing the diplomatic cables (Chaiken). While this poll claims to represent the entire “public,” summing over the utilities of this many commoners still seems incalculable. Furthermore, acts of vigilantism often result in unexpected consequences; the variability of consequences of vigilantism makes utilitarianism an inconsistent standard of morality.
